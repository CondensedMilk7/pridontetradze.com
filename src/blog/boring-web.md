The title does seem like a boomer reminiscing the old days, but I wasn't around back then —
When the internet was mostly full of plain text documents for privileged people.
By privilege I mean owning a computer, having access to the internet and an technical ability to actually use it.
I do believe that information is a basic human right and every piece of academic knowledge should be public.
Nevertheless, pursuit of knowledge, curiosity, or contemplative leisure is not why most people use the internet.
Nowadays, the web is an engagement machine that satisfies basic human compulsions, much like smoking.
You scroll between every free time that emerges between every moment of work or rest,
when you don't know what to do with your hands.
I won't go through all the lengths of painting villains of the digital era, we see and hear enough of that.
Instead, I want to take a look into how the internet was used back in the day and explore a way of using
this World Wide Waste of a web we have now in a similar old fashion,
in a utilitarian, or better yet, _ascetic_ way. So that we can read the web like we read a book.

## The old web

The old web was all about academic papers. Just static text documents pointing to other documents,
for fellow academics to find and share.
At the beginning there were no images either.
You were to view the document from the command-line terminal.
You can even [try the line-mode simulator](https://line-mode.cern.ch/www/hypertext/WWW/TheProject.html)
to get a feel for what it was like.
In short, it was a interconnected library primarily for researching and sharing data.
Later came more elaborate user interfaces, with web-browsers that could even render the images in the same document.
"The Mosaic Revolution" — they called it.

Among the earliest websites were:

- [info.cern.ch](https://info.cern.ch), the instruction manual for the web itself;
- [Trojan room coffe pot](https://www.cl.cam.ac.uk/coffee/coffee.html), a solution for Cambridge researchers not having to go downstairs only to find an empty coffee pot (peak software right there);
- [IMDb](https://www.imdb.com/), internet's first movie database;
- [GeoCities (link to archive)](https://geocities.restorativland.org/), where people made their personal homepages.

Compare it to the modern web when you can't even tell if it's the humans making the posts or commenting on them.
Synthesized voices narrating the meaningless events on a video, as if you don't see what's happening yourself.
Advertisements and cookie consent banners popping up on every page visit, video stream and reel scroll.

In the old internet, something interesting _had to be found_, so you had to search, nay, _surf_ the web.
Curiosities would come up either by word of mouth, or by a deliberate search, something you would do when you are bored.
The content itself was boring too. Either research papers, or some people sharing their two cents about things.
Interesting, true, but boring.
Why would such a boring thing even be worthwhile?

## Boring is good

I the use word **boring** in a very particular sense. What by today's standards is understood as not entertaining, stimulating and engaging.
Something not falling within the 10 second reel criteria.
Young people today struggle to watch movies in their entirety,
which was considered the most approachable and engaging form of media a couple of decades ago.
I find myself in the similar situation every now and then.
Especially during work, when my AI agent has to do the heavy-lifting of writing code and I am a little too tired and too bored
to do anything else but scroll mindlessly on my phone.

For some reason we are in a hurry to enjoy things, and to do things in general.
What are we in such a rush for?
For getting the joy quickly out of the way so we can focus on work?
For providing a better value to the stakeholders?
What is the end goal for such a fast-paced way of living?
Are we speedrunning life?
[Slow down.](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=cpckQFjvntw)

Think back  to the wonderful conversation you have had with a friend late at night,
while you had nothing better to do.
I am sure every person has had a similar experience at least to some extent.
Or perhaps look back into childhood, where you had nothing to do but play a video game,
where half of the gameplay was just walking.
There's a reason people remembering fondly such simple moments in their lives.
Perhaps in childhood the best book you have ever read was the one you picked up because you were bored out of your mind
and you had nothing better to do? The book that became a formative factor in the way you think and do things.
Perhaps I am extremely lucky, but I have experienced all of the above and all of them stemmed from boredom.

In boredom, you let your senses and perception become more sensitive to even the most mundane things.
It creates a very special phenomenological field where time is experienced at its fullest.
Minutes can feel like hours.
Therefore what you experience in such moments seem to be suspended in time.
All the wonderful things I have learned, felt or experienced that I carry with me to this day,
often times, have been in boredom.

I have found that years in tech industry have had their toll on me. My spirit has become numb, weak, and hungry.
Mainly because I am stuck with building digital machines for living.
Even though development is fun, it deprives the soul.
I find myself drawn philosophy and theology, just like I was keen on psychology back in my university years,
albeit with more humility this time.
I find that my attention span and discipline is much weaker than it was. It's funny that a more immature version of me could handle more,
but I have trouble reading, writing or conversing for extended periods.

Therefore it is necessary for one to revitalize the attention span that is so precious, yet so easily given up.
It is only natural that I would want to purify the rest of the interactions I have with the digital world
(apart from work) and avoid the same lifeless engagement-churning machines that I build to make the ends meet.

## Make the web boring

I have recently taken to, let's call it, _digital asceticism_.
This means I don't engage with short-term entertainment content.
It is simpler and less cool than it sounds (because it makes me feel old), but it works wonders.

Let it be known that I am not changing my experience of the web for the sake of productivity.
I don't believe in productivity. A human is not a production machine that outputs value.
A human being is a spirit that _wills_ and _contemplates_.
I believe that such a state of being arises precisely in boredom.
Therefore to maximize this boredom that gives ground to such a wonderful human experience,
we must make the web boring.

Here are the steps I have taken:

- Bring social media usage down to an **absolute minimum**. I am obviously incapable of being a hermit in a cave (yet), so I still use web for chatting and calling, but that's the point — free time should be away from meaningless content and towards connecting with people you care about.
- **Fewer forms of media**. I stick to text and music. If it has to be a video, it must be a movie or a long-format video.
- **Avoid short content**, so every video or a movie I get to watch is a commitment, therefore it has to be meaningful and good. Every article or a book I get to read, likewise, has to be interesting and insightful.
- I just **don't use web** if I can help it. It's really that simple. Go out with friends, take a hike, find mushrooms, catch fish.

## Concluding notes

Slowly but surely I am regaining my capacity to enjoy boredom. To do nothing, or do simple things,
like reading a book, going out for a walk, cuddling with my cats or just sitting out on the porch and drinking coffee for an hour.
In return, I feel like I have taken the best sleep I've ever had mentally.
All that because the internet for me has become like a library. If I mean to use it, I have to commit to something worthwhile.
