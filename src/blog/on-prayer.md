## The misunderstanding

Turns out, since I was a kid, up until a year or so ago, I had a completely pagan understanding of prayer.
Perhaps it is true that many people, even Christians, still retain this kind of view on prayer,
but you cannot blame them as it is the most ancient and instinctual way of knowing greater forces.
Especially the kind of forces, that seemingly come from nowhere, like a natural disaster,
angry and ferocious, like a wild beast tearing apart the livelihood of a primitive man.

In pagan faith, deities are worshipped transactionally.
Gods are like harsh rulers demanding a tax, in return for peace.
Pagans observe the forces to be at times ruthless, at times peaceful,
and think of a way to win favor of these forces.
They offer whatever valuable they have.
A man demands something from the deities,
and in return they expect the deity to be satisfied in receiving sacrifice and worship.

In such a pagan sense, what is a prayer, if not a plea for a favor?
And to an uneducated mind, a Christian's prayer to God seems no different.
It is my understanding that many do even pray in that manner,
they are sometimes even bold enough to offer something to God in return.
An offer, a sacrifice to an all-loving and all-powerful God?
That might seem no less absurd than a prayer in church or in front of icons —
cannot God hear and know everything and everywhere?
Then why does it seem like a praying person is begging for God's attention?

This naturally gives rise to more questions,
such as: Why did Jesus Christ Himself pray? Why would God (in essence) pray to Himself?

## The true purpose

God does demand a sacrifice for salvation. Perhaps the greatest one than any false deity would.
Jesus teaches us to give up **everything** — one's life and the whole world:

> Then Jesus said to his disciples,
> "Whoever wants to be my disciple must deny themselves and take up their cross and follow me.
> For whoever wants to save their life will lose it, but whoever loses their life for me will find it.
> What good will it be for someone to gain the whole world, yet forfeit their soul?"
>
> — Matthew 16:24-26

To live in Christ, is to declare [death to the world](https://deathtotheworld.com/).
So every waking day of a Christian is a battlefield, every denial of the world — a sacrifice.
Of course, to consistently win such a battle, is a different matter altogether.
Nevertheless, it is clear that to be saved, one must turn away from the momentary pleasures of the world,
often times to even give up the safety and peace of mind,
as Christians were (and even to this day in some places are) violently persecuted for their faith.
This means that prayer in a Christian sense cannot be understood as the bargain between the mortals and God.
God does not promise peace in this world in exchange for sacrifice.

The scriptures shed great deal of light to why and how one should pray.
Take for example "The Lord's Prayer" from Matthew 6:9-10:

> "Our Father in heaven, hallowed be your name, your kingdom come, your will be done, on earth as it is in heaven."

It is a declaration of submission to God's will and, by extension, an attempt or yearning towards God's grace.

> "This is the confidence we have in approaching God: that if we ask anything according to his will, he hears us."
>
> — 1 John 5:14:

It is not that a praying man is attempting to persuade God,
but rather that one prays within the boundaries of His will.
This allows the soul to draw near Him.
It is both the attempt to move towards,
and the ongoing result of being anchored in Christ.

As Paul states, you let the Holy Spirit work within you to help you be uplifted towards God.

> "In the same way, the Spirit helps us in our weakness.
> We do not know what we ought to pray for, but the Spirit himself intercedes for us through wordless groans.
> And he who searches our hearts knows the mind of the Spirit,
> because the Spirit intercedes for God’s people in accordance with the will of God."
> 
> — Romans 8:26-27

It is therefore the reason, why Jesus Christ prays to the Father.
While fully God, being fully human requires willful attempt of aligning with the God's will.
Jesus declares to the Father, that he wishes the Father's will to be done:

> "My Father, if it is possible, may this cup be taken from me. Yet not as I will, but as you will."
>
> — Matthew 26:39

## Concluding notes

What helped me fathom the purpose of prayer in its simplest form
was "The Divine Names" by Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite.
Namely the analogy he uses to describe prayer:

> "Or picture ourselves aboard a boat. There are hawsers \[ropes\] joining it to some rock.
> We take hold of them and pull on them,
> and it is as if we were dragging the rock to us when in fact we are hauling ourselves and our boat toward that rock."
>
> — The Divine Names, Ch. 3.1

God is immutable. He does not move toward us or away from us in response to prayer. He is everywhere.
Everything is _"of him and to him"_.
Therefore the prayer is _for the pray-er_.
It is the rope that allows us to pull ourselves towards God.
